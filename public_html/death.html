Death rule by Elisabeth Kubler Ross



In 1969 the Swiss-born psychiatrist Elisabeth Kübler-Ross conceptualized five stages in facing one’s terminal illness: denial, anger, bargaining, depression, and acceptance. Although most thanatologists accept the Kübler-Ross stages, they also recognize that these stages occur neither with predictable regularity nor in any set order.

They are part of the framework that makes up our learning to live with the one we lost. They are tools to help us frame and identify what we may be feeling. But they are not stops on some linear timeline in grief.

Elisabeth Kübler-Ross, an American psychiatrist, has outlined the stages through which people, informed of their own approaching death, are said to pass. Her writings are based on a wide but essentially American experience.
